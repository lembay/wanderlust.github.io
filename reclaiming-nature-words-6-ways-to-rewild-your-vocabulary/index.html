Reclaiming Nature Words: 6 Ways to Rewild Your Vocabulary

By rewilding your vocabulary with these six approaches, you not only deepen your lexicon but also foster a more intimate and informed relationship with the natural world. Words have the power to shape our perceptions and actions, and a rich vocabulary of nature words can inspire both reverence and responsibility towards the earth.

April 10, 2024


In an age where digital landscapes often overshadow the natural world, the words we use to describe our environment can significantly impact our connection to it. “Reclaiming Nature Words” is about enriching our language with terms that draw us closer to the earth and its wonders. This article explores six ways to rewild your vocabulary, fostering a deeper bond with nature.

Embrace Indigenous Languages

Embrace Indigenous Languages by integrating native terms into your everyday language. Indigenous cultures have words that encapsulate complex ecological phenomena or relationships within the natural world, offering a nuanced perspective that mainstream languages sometimes lack. Learning these terms not only broadens your vocabulary but also pays respect to the wisdom of cultures deeply connected to the land.

Dive into Nature Poetry

Dive into Nature Poetry and literature to discover a treasure trove of evocative words and phrases that capture the essence of the natural world. Poets and writers like Mary Oliver and John Muir use language that paints vivid pictures of landscapes, flora, and fauna, inspiring readers to see nature through a more appreciative and detailed lens.

Use Specificity in Descriptions

Use Specificity in Descriptions to enrich your narrative of the natural world. Instead of generic terms like “bird” or “tree,” delve deeper into species and varieties. Identifying a “scarlet tanager” or a “weeping willow” not only enhances your vocabulary but also your observational skills, encouraging a closer look at nature’s diversity.

Adopt Forgotten Words

Adopt Forgotten Words from the lexicon of nature that have fallen out of everyday use. Words like “petrichor,” the scent of earth after rain, or “brontide,” the distant rumble of thunder, evoke specific sensory experiences tied to the natural world. Reviving such terms can rekindle an appreciation for the subtleties of our environment.

Engage with Nature Journals

Engage with Nature Journals and documentaries that offer a rich source of specialized vocabulary related to ecosystems, geography, and wildlife. Such resources can introduce terms that provide a more accurate and vivid description of natural phenomena, enriching your conversations and writings about the outdoors.

Participate in Citizen Science Projects

Participate in Citizen Science Projects to learn terminology related to biodiversity, conservation, and ecological research. These projects not only contribute to scientific knowledge but also immerse you in the language of ecology, helping you articulate the complex interconnections within natural habitats.
